The Harsh Reality

Recruiters are human. They're also overworked. When a job posting gets 200 applications, they spend 6 seconds per resume deciding: "Possible fit" or "No." This isn't malice‚Äîit's triage.

In those 6 seconds, they scan (not read) looking for:

  • Name, title, years of experience
  • Obvious red flags or yellow flags
  • Keywords matching the job
  • Recent, relevant experience
  • Any reason to say "no"

What Happens in 6 Seconds:

  • 0-1 sec
    Scan header - Name, title, location
  • 1-3 sec
    Check experience - Most recent role, company names, tenure
  • 3-5 sec
    Look for keywords - Do I see "Python," "Google," or other job requirements?
  • 5-6 sec
    Make decision - Yes, maybe, or reject pile

Red Flags That Kill Your Resume Instantly

  • Resume is 3+ pages - Recruiters assume you can't be concise
  • Vague job titles - "Team Member," "Associate" without context
  • No metrics or results - Generic descriptions signal weak impact
  • Outdated design or formatting - Looks unprofessional or uses outdated software
  • Missing job keywords - Doesn't match job description language
  • Employment gaps without explanation - Raises questions
  • Typos or grammatical errors - Signals carelessness
üí° Pro Tip: Your resume is a marketing document, not a biography. Every word should answer "Why are you great for this job?"

How to Win the 6-Second Screen

  • Lead with your best fit - Put most relevant experience first
  • Use the job description as a cheat sheet - Mirror language where honest
  • Make metrics visible - "Increased revenue 40%" beats "managed initiatives"
  • Master the headline - Job title + years experience + key specialty
  • Cut ruthlessly - Keep to 1 page if possible, max 2 pages
  • Bold important keywords - Makes scanning easier for recruiter eyes
  • Test with a friend - Can they tell you your fit in 6 seconds?

The Transformation

Before: "Responsible for managing team projects and contributing to company goals"

After: "Led cross-functional team of 5 to deliver Q3 project 2 weeks early, saving $50K in costs"

Same person, different framing. The second one survives the 6-second screen.
