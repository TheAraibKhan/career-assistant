The Reality

What actually happens in those 6 seconds

Recruiters aren't reading on first glance — they're scanning for visual anchors: your name, most recent role, company names, and whether the layout makes key information findable in seconds.

If those things don't surface immediately, the resume gets set aside — not because of your experience, but because the presentation didn't do its job. This check identifies exactly those weak spots.

1
Header and contact info — Is your name prominent? Is your current city visible? Is your LinkedIn link easy to spot?
2
Most recent role — What company, what title, how long? These answer "is this person roughly right?" in under 2 seconds.
3
Achievement bullets — Do they lead with impact? Numbers and results catch the eye; vague responsibilities get skipped.
4
Visual clarity — Is this easy to read? Dense text and poor spacing move a resume to the "later" pile that never gets revisited.
Writing for the 6-Second Scan

Small wording changes, big visible difference

The difference between resumes that get callbacks often comes down to how the first line of each bullet reads. Weak verbs and vague language are invisible; strong, specific openings make recruiters slow down.

Before
Helped with development of features for the team's main product and assisted in supporting users
After
Built 3 core product features shipped to 14,000 users, reducing support tickets by 22%
  • Start every bullet with a strong verb — Built, reduced, led, launched — not "helped" or "assisted with"
  • Add one number per bullet where you can — Scale, percentage, timeframe, team size — any figure adds instant credibility
  • Put your strongest bullet first — Recruiters rarely read all bullets; the first one carries the most weight
  • Keep bullets to one or two lines — Long paragraphs get skimmed or skipped entirely in the 6-second window
