The Hidden Problem

Why resumes fail before they're even read

ATS software isn't sophisticated. It reads resumes the way a plain text parser would — top to bottom, left to right. Tables, graphics, unusual columns, and custom fonts all break that flow. The system marks those resumes as unreadable and moves on.

The frustrating part? Your qualifications were never the issue. The file itself was the problem. That's what this check catches.

  • Tables and multi-column layouts — ATS reads text sequentially and gets scrambled by table cells
  • Images, logos, or headshots — Visual elements are invisible to text parsers and create gaps in your profile
  • Text in headers or footers — Contact information placed in document headers is commonly lost entirely
  • Decorative or non-standard fonts — Fancy typefaces fail to parse correctly and may appear as garbled characters
  • Missing job-specific keywords — ATS filters by exact terms; if the language doesn't match, you're invisible
Why It Matters

A technical problem, not a qualification problem

The hardest part about an ATS rejection is that you'll never know it happened. You send an application, hear nothing, and assume you weren't qualified enough. In reality, your resume may have been discarded in seconds — not because of your experience, but because of how your file was structured.

Fixing ATS compatibility is the single highest-leverage change you can make to your resume. Everything else — your bullet points, your keywords, your achievements — only matters if your resume actually gets read first.

How to Fix It

Simple changes that make a real difference

You don't need to redesign your resume from scratch. Most ATS issues come down to a handful of structural choices that are easy to correct once you know what to look for.

  • Use a single-column layout — Bullet points and clean section headings parse perfectly across all ATS platforms
  • Remove all images and icons — Including profile photos, skill bar graphics, and logo images
  • Choose standard fonts — Arial, Calibri, Georgia, or Times New Roman are universally supported
  • Put contact info in the body — Never in the document header or footer, where parsers often skip it
  • Mirror the job description's language — Use the exact terms the employer uses, not synonyms
  • Save as .docx or a clean .pdf — Both are well-supported; avoid .pages or unusual export formats

Quick ATS Compatibility Checklist

No images, logos, or any embedded graphics
No tables used for layout or skills sections
Contact details are in the document body, not the file header
Standard font (11–12pt), consistent throughout
Keywords from the job posting appear in natural context
File saved as .docx or a text-selectable .pdf
